What Is A Shoulder Injury Claim?

A shoulder injury claim is a way to seek compensation for pain and suffering caused by an accident. Accidents could happen in the workplace, on the road or in a public place.

In order to be eligible to claim compensation, you must be able to show that another party had a duty of care to ensure your safety and that they failed to meet (breached) this duty.

Eligibility Criteria
  1. Someone owed you a duty of care. This could be your employer, a road user or the occupier of a public place.
  2. This party breached their duty of care to you. For example, they may have failed to pay due care and attention whilst driving.
  3. You suffered an injury as a direct result of this breach.

If your case meets these criteria, you may be eligible to claim compensation.

What Common Shoulder Injuries Could I Claim For?

The shoulder is a complex joint moving the arm through different planes of motion. It is made up of the ball and socket joint, shoulder muscles such as the rotator cuff and connective tissues. Damage to any of these could cause constant or recurring pain, disability or the loss of use of the shoulder.

Examples of shoulder injuries include:

  • A rotator cuff tear
  • Damaged nerves
  • A dislocated shoulder
  • Torn cartilage or ligaments
  • A frozen shoulder
  • Bursitis
  • A fractured clavicle
  • Arthritis
  • Damage to the brachial plexus

A shoulder injury could cause a variety of symptoms including pain and swelling, bruising and tingling. It could also lead to reduced or lost movement in the arm.

What Types Of Accidents Could Lead To A Claim?

You could claim for a shoulder injury at work, on the road or in a public place. In any of these instances, you must clearly show that someone else had a duty of care to you which they breached.

Accidents At Work

Under the Health and Safety at Work etc Act 1974, your employer owes you a duty of care. To meet this, they must take reasonable steps to ensure your safety. This might include conducting risk assessments, providing personal protective equipment (PPE) and ensuring machinery is safe to use.

  • Being hit by a falling object from a height due to inadequate safety measures
  • Slipping or tripping on an unmarked hazard and landing on your shoulder
  • Repetitive strain injuries from lifting or carrying without proper training
  • Machinery accidents caused by faulty or poorly maintained equipment

Road Traffic Accidents

All road users owe each other a duty of care. If a driver, cyclist or other road user breaches this duty and you sustain a shoulder injury as a result, you could make a claim for compensation.

  • Whiplash and shoulder injuries from a rear-end collision
  • Shoulder fractures caused by a side-impact crash
  • Injuries from being hit as a pedestrian or cyclist
  • Dislocations caused by a motorbike accident

Accidents In Public Places

Under the Occupiers' Liability Act 1957, occupiers of public places have a duty to ensure that visitors are reasonably safe. If they fail to do so and you suffer a shoulder injury, you could claim compensation.

  • Slipping on a wet floor without warning signs in a shop
  • Tripping over damaged pavement maintained by a local council
  • Falling due to poor lighting in a public car park

What Evidence Could Help Me Make A Shoulder Injury Claim?

Personal injury claims will typically be made against the insurance provider of the party liable for your injury. To satisfy them that the claim is valid, you will need to prove the injury was caused by a third party breaching a duty of care they owed you.

  • Photographs and CCTV footage. Your accident may have been captured on CCTV or you may have been able to take photos of the scene and/or your injuries.
  • Copies of accident reports. If injured in a public place or at work the accident should have been recorded in an accident book. If injured on the roads, the accident will have been reported to the police.
  • Witness contact details. Witness statements can help to provide corroborating evidence of what happened.
  • Medical reports. You can request a copy of your medical records from the NHS. These may detail what injury you sustained and how it has impacted you.

Additionally, if you experienced financial losses related to your shoulder injury, you will need to provide proof of these losses. Examples include copies of financial statements, wage slips and invoices.

Shoulder Injury Compensation Payouts

If your shoulder injury claim is successful, you will be awarded compensation. Shoulder injury compensation claims could be awarded two types of damages — general damages and special damages.

General damages may be calculated by using guidelines from the Judicial College (JC). The JC contains guideline amounts on how much may be awarded for different forms of injury and different severities.

Can I Claim For Loss Of Earnings?

Special damages are designed to compensate you for financial losses associated with your injuries. If you were unable to work you could claim compensation for lost income.

Additionally, special damages could compensate you for:

  • The cost of prescription medication
  • The cost of medical treatment, such as private medical care including physiotherapy
  • Childcare costs if you are unable to care for your child during recovery
  • The cost of travelling to and from medical appointments
  • The cost of care and help in the home

How Long Do I Have To Claim For A Shoulder Injury?

Important: 3-Year Time Limit

You must make your shoulder injury claim within 3 years of the date of injury. After this deadline passes, you may lose your right to claim compensation entirely. Don't delay — contact us today for a free assessment.

The standard time limit for shoulder injury claims is set by the Limitation Act 1980. Where a child or a person who lacks the mental capacity to make a claim has suffered harm, the time limit is suspended.

In the case of a child, the time limit does not begin until their 18th birthday. The period then lasts until they turn 21. Where someone lacks mental capacity to claim, the time limit will not apply unless and until they regain their capacity.

In either of these instances, another party may act as a litigation friend in the claim — making the claim on behalf of the person injured.
